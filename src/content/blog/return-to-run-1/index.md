A year later I broke my ankle on a trail run.

A Weber A fracture of the distal fibula — the lowest classification of lateral ankle fracture, stable, and fortunately one of the faster fractures to recover from. I didn't know any of that at the time. I just knew I heard a pop and my run was over.

Six weeks, every Tuesday, I'm documenting how I'm planning my return to running using Chiron — the training platform I've been building for coaches and competitive runners.

---

## The diagnosis

A Weber A fracture sits at the level of the ankle joint or below. Because the syndesmosis — the ligament connecting the tibia and fibula — is typically intact, the fracture is stable and conservative management is usually sufficient. No surgery. A moonboot, then progressive loading.

My sports doctor Dr Peter Friis confirmed it was healing well at day 10 X-ray. By Week 3 I had transitioned from a moonboot to an AirCast Air Stirrup and was beginning to load carefully.

![Day 10 X-ray showing Weber A distal fibula fracture with fracture sites circled](./xray-day-10-weber-a-fracture.jpeg)
*Day 10 X-ray — Weber A fracture of the distal fibula. Stable, healing well.*

## The team

I'm working with my physio, [Brad Beer](https://instagram.com/brad_beer), APA Sports Physiotherapist, to plan the return. At this stage of recovery, restoring ankle range of motion takes precedence over bone loading or maintaining aerobic fitness.

In the meantime, contralateral training — exercises on the uninjured side — is helping maintain some conditioning and preserve neural pathways while the fracture heals.

![Fitting the Aircast air stirrup brace in the home gym](./aircast-in-gym.jpg)
